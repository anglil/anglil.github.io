Chapter 1Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion without regard to the forces that cause it. We develop the vocabulary of displacement, velocity, and acceleration, first in one dimension, then in two and three dimensions. The chapter concludes with projectile motion and relative-motion problems characteristic of competition physics.

1.1 Position and Displacement

Definition 1.1 — Position Vector

The position of a particle at time $t$ is specified by the vector $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ in a chosen coordinate system. The displacement over the interval $[t_1, t_2]$ is

$$\Delta\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1).$$

Remark. Displacement is a vector; distance is the scalar $\displaystyle s = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} |\mathbf{v}(t)|\,dt$, which is always $\geq |\Delta\mathbf{r}|$.

1.2 Velocity

Definition 1.2 — Velocity

The instantaneous velocity is the time derivative of the position vector:

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \dot{x}\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + \dot{y}\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + \dot{z}\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}.$$

The average velocity over $[t_1,t_2]$ is $\overline{\mathbf{v}} = \Delta\mathbf{r}/\Delta t$. The speed is $v = |\mathbf{v}|$.

1.3 Acceleration

Definition 1.3 — Acceleration
$$\mathbf{a}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2}.$$

1.4 Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Theorem 1.1 — SUVAT Equations

For constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$ with initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$ and initial position $\mathbf{r}_0$:

$$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}\,t$$ $$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0\,t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}\,t^2$$ $$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2\,\mathbf{a}\cdot\Delta\mathbf{r}$$ $$\Delta\mathbf{r} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{v})\,t$$
Proof

Integrate $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ from $0$ to $t$:

$$\int_0^t \mathbf{a}\,dt' = \mathbf{v}(t) - \mathbf{v}_0 \implies \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{a}\,t.$$

Integrate once more: $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_0 + \int_0^t \mathbf{v}\,dt' = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}t^2$.

Eliminate $t$ between the first two equations: from the first, $t = (v - v_0)/a$. Substituting into the displacement equation (in the direction of motion) gives $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$. $\blacksquare$

Example 1.1 — Free-Fall with Air Resistance Onset

A ball is dropped from rest at height $h = 45\,\text{m}$. Ignoring air resistance, find (a) the time to reach the ground, (b) the speed at impact.

Solution

(a) Take downward as positive. $h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \implies t = \sqrt{2h/g} = \sqrt{2(45)/9.8} \approx 3.03\,\text{s}$.

(b) $v = gt = 9.8 \times 3.03 \approx 29.7\,\text{m/s}$.

1.5 Projectile Motion

Decompose the motion into horizontal (no acceleration) and vertical (acceleration $g$ downward) components.

Theorem 1.2 — Projectile Range and Maximum Height

A projectile launched from ground level with speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal:

$$R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}, \qquad H = \frac{v_0^2 \sin^2\theta}{2g}, \qquad T = \frac{2v_0\sin\theta}{g}.$$

The range is maximized at $\theta = 45°$.

Proof

Let $v_{0x} = v_0\cos\theta$, $v_{0y} = v_0\sin\theta$.

Flight time: $0 = v_{0y}T - \frac{1}{2}gT^2 \implies T = 2v_{0y}/g$.

Range: $R = v_{0x}T = v_0\cos\theta \cdot 2v_0\sin\theta/g = v_0^2\sin 2\theta/g$.

Max height: at apex, $v_y = 0 \implies t^* = v_{0y}/g$, so $H = v_{0y}t^* - \frac{1}{2}g(t^*)^2 = v_0^2\sin^2\theta/(2g)$. $\blacksquare$

Example 1.2 — Projectile on an Incline

A particle is launched with speed $v_0$ at angle $\alpha$ above an inclined plane of angle $\beta$. Find the range along the incline.

Solution

Use tilted coordinates: $x'$ along the incline, $y'$ perpendicular. Then $a_{x'} = -g\sin\beta$ and $a_{y'} = -g\cos\beta$.

Setting $y' = 0$: $T = 2v_0\sin\alpha / (g\cos\beta)$.

Range along incline: $L = v_0\cos\alpha \cdot T + \frac{1}{2}(-g\sin\beta)T^2$. Substituting:

$$L = \frac{2v_0^2\sin\alpha}{g\cos^2\beta}\bigl(\cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta\bigr) = \frac{2v_0^2\sin\alpha\cos(\alpha+\beta)}{g\cos^2\beta}.$$

1.6 Relative Motion

Theorem 1.3 — Galilean Velocity Transformation

If frame $S'$ moves with constant velocity $\mathbf{V}$ relative to frame $S$, then for any particle:

$$\mathbf{v}_S = \mathbf{v}_{S'} + \mathbf{V}, \qquad \mathbf{a}_S = \mathbf{a}_{S'}.$$
Example 1.3 — River Crossing

A swimmer can swim at speed $v_s$ in still water. A river of width $d$ flows with speed $v_r < v_s$. What heading minimizes the crossing time, and what heading makes the swimmer arrive directly opposite?

Solution

Minimum time: Swim perpendicular to the bank. Time $= d/v_s$, but the swimmer is swept downstream by $v_r d/v_s$.

Straight across: Aim upstream at angle $\theta$ with $v_s\sin\theta = v_r$, i.e. $\theta = \arcsin(v_r/v_s)$. The perpendicular component is $v_s\cos\theta = \sqrt{v_s^2 - v_r^2}$, so $t = d/\sqrt{v_s^2 - v_r^2}$.

1.7 Curvilinear Coordinates

In polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ the position, velocity, and acceleration are:

$$\mathbf{r} = r\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$ $$\mathbf{v} = \dot{r}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}} + r\dot{\theta}\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$ $$\mathbf{a} = (\ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2)\,\hat{\mathbf{r}} + (r\ddot{\theta} + 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta})\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$

The term $r\dot{\theta}^2$ is the centripetal acceleration; $2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}$ is the Coriolis-like cross term that appears when $r$ is changing.

Definition 1.4 — Tangential and Normal Components

For motion along a curve, the acceleration decomposes as:

$$\mathbf{a} = \frac{dv}{dt}\,\hat{\mathbf{t}} + \frac{v^2}{\rho}\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{t}}$ is the unit tangent, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ the unit normal pointing toward the center of curvature, and $\rho$ is the radius of curvature.

1.8 Exercises

Exercise 1.1

A particle moves along the $x$-axis with velocity $v(t) = v_0(1 - t/\tau)$ for $0 \leq t \leq 2\tau$. Find (a) the displacement over $[0, 2\tau]$, (b) the total distance traveled.

Exercise 1.2

A ball is thrown from the top of a building of height $H$ with horizontal speed $v_0$. At the same instant, a second ball is thrown from the ground directly below with speed $u$ at angle $\theta$ above the horizontal. Find the condition on $u$ and $\theta$ for the balls to collide in mid-air.

Exercise 1.3

Two particles start simultaneously from the same point. Particle A moves with constant speed $v_A$ along a straight line. Particle B always moves directly toward A with constant speed $v_B > v_A$. Find the time at which B catches A.

Exercise 1.4

A projectile is launched at speed $v_0$ at angle $\theta$ on a planet where gravitational acceleration decreases linearly with altitude: $g(h) = g_0(1 - h/R)$, where $R \gg H_{\max}$. Find the first-order correction to the range.

Exercise 1.5

A boat crosses a river of width $d$ in which the current velocity varies as $v(y) = v_0 \sin(\pi y / d)$ where $y$ is the distance from one bank. If the boat has speed $u$ relative to the water, find the heading angle $\alpha(y)$ as a function of position such that the boat arrives directly opposite, and compute the crossing time.
