Chapter 4Momentum and Collisions

4.1 Linear Momentum and Impulse

Definition 4.1 — Linear Momentum
$$\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}.$$
Theorem 4.1 — Impulse–Momentum Theorem
$$\mathbf{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{F}\,dt = \Delta\mathbf{p}.$$
Theorem 4.2 — Conservation of Momentum

If the net external force on a system is zero, the total momentum is conserved: $\sum_i m_i \mathbf{v}_i = \text{const}$.

4.2 Center of Mass

Definition 4.2 — Center of Mass
$$\mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum_i m_i \mathbf{r}_i}{\sum_i m_i}, \qquad \mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}}{M_{\text{total}}}.$$

Newton's second law for the system: $\mathbf{F}_{\text{ext}} = M\mathbf{a}_{\text{cm}}$. The center of mass moves as if the total mass were concentrated there, acted on by the net external force.

4.3 Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

In all collisions, momentum is conserved. Elastic collisions also conserve kinetic energy. Perfectly inelastic collisions result in the objects sticking together, maximizing kinetic energy loss.

Theorem 4.3 — 1D Elastic Collision

For a head-on elastic collision between masses $m_1$ (velocity $u_1$) and $m_2$ (at rest):

$$v_1 = \frac{m_1 - m_2}{m_1 + m_2}\,u_1, \qquad v_2 = \frac{2m_1}{m_1 + m_2}\,u_1.$$
Proof

Momentum: $m_1 u_1 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$. Energy: $m_1 u_1^2 = m_1 v_1^2 + m_2 v_2^2$.

From momentum: $m_1(u_1 - v_1) = m_2 v_2$. From energy: $m_1(u_1^2 - v_1^2) = m_2 v_2^2$, i.e., $m_1(u_1 - v_1)(u_1 + v_1) = m_2 v_2^2$.

Dividing: $u_1 + v_1 = v_2$. Substituting back gives the result. $\blacksquare$

Example 4.1 — Ballistic Pendulum

A bullet of mass $m$ embeds in a block of mass $M$ suspended by a string. The block rises to height $h$. Find the bullet's speed.

Solution

Collision (momentum): $mv_0 = (m+M)V$. Swing (energy): $\frac{1}{2}(m+M)V^2 = (m+M)gh$.

So $V = \sqrt{2gh}$ and $v_0 = \frac{m+M}{m}\sqrt{2gh}$.

4.4 Two-Dimensional Collisions

In 2D, apply conservation of $p_x$ and $p_y$ separately. For elastic collisions, add $\Delta K = 0$. A key result: in an elastic collision of equal masses where one is initially at rest, the final velocities are perpendicular.

4.5 The Center-of-Mass Frame

In the CM frame, the total momentum is zero. Elastic collisions in the CM frame simply reverse each particle's velocity direction (in 1D) or change direction by an angle $\chi$ (in 2D, the scattering angle).

4.6 Variable-Mass Systems

Theorem 4.4 — Rocket Equation (Tsiolkovsky)

For a rocket expelling mass at rate $|\dot{m}|$ with exhaust velocity $v_e$ relative to the rocket:

$$m\frac{dv}{dt} = -v_e\,\dot{m} + F_{\text{ext}}$$

In gravity-free space with no external forces, $\Delta v = v_e \ln(m_0/m_f)$.

4.7 Exercises

Exercise 4.1

$n$ identical balls are arranged in a line on a frictionless surface. The first is given velocity $v_0$. All collisions are elastic. Show that the last ball departs with speed $v_0$ and all others remain at rest.

Exercise 4.2

A ball bounces vertically between two horizontal surfaces separated by height $h$. Each bounce is elastic. Find the average force on the lower surface if the ball has mass $m$.

Exercise 4.3

A particle of mass $m$ and speed $v$ strikes a stationary particle of mass $M$ elastically. Find the scattering angle in the lab frame as a function of the CM scattering angle $\chi$ and the mass ratio $m/M$.

Exercise 4.4

A chain of length $L$ and mass $M$ lies coiled on a table. One end is pulled vertically upward with constant velocity $v$. Find the force applied as a function of the length $y$ already lifted.
