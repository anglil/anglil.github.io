Chapter 2Newton's Laws and Dynamics

This chapter presents the three laws of motion, the concept of force, and systematic methods for solving dynamics problems including free-body diagrams, constraint equations, and non-inertial reference frames.

2.1 Newton's Three Laws

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

In an inertial reference frame, a body remains at rest or in uniform rectilinear motion unless acted upon by a net external force.

Newton's Second Law

In an inertial frame, the net force on a particle equals the rate of change of its momentum:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = \frac{d(m\mathbf{v})}{dt}.$$

For constant mass: $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = m\mathbf{a}$.

Newton's Third Law

If body A exerts a force $\mathbf{F}_{AB}$ on body B, then B exerts a force $\mathbf{F}_{BA} = -\mathbf{F}_{AB}$ on A. The forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different bodies.

2.2 Common Forces

Gravity near Earth's surface: $\mathbf{W} = -mg\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}$.

Normal force $\mathbf{N}$: perpendicular to the contact surface, adjusts to prevent interpenetration.

Friction: Static friction $f_s \leq \mu_s N$; kinetic friction $f_k = \mu_k N$, opposing the direction of sliding.

Tension $T$: along an ideal (massless, inextensible) string, equal on both sides when passing over a frictionless pulley.

Spring force: $F = -kx$ (Hooke's law), where $x$ is the extension from natural length.

2.3 Free-Body Diagrams and Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify the system (the object or set of objects).
  2. Draw all external forces on the system.
  3. Choose a coordinate system (often aligned with the acceleration).
  4. Write $\sum F_x = ma_x$ and $\sum F_y = ma_y$.
  5. Add constraint equations (e.g., string length, rolling without slipping).
  6. Solve the resulting system of equations.
Example 2.1 — Atwood Machine

Two masses $m_1 > m_2$ hang from an ideal string over an ideal pulley. Find the acceleration and tension.

Solution

Let $a$ be the acceleration (downward for $m_1$, upward for $m_2$). For $m_1$: $m_1 g - T = m_1 a$. For $m_2$: $T - m_2 g = m_2 a$.

Adding: $(m_1 - m_2)g = (m_1 + m_2)a$, so

$$a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2}, \qquad T = \frac{2m_1 m_2 g}{m_1 + m_2}.$$

2.4 Inclined Planes and Friction

Example 2.2 — Block on a Rough Incline

A block of mass $m$ sits on a plane inclined at angle $\theta$ with coefficient of static friction $\mu_s$. Find the critical angle for sliding.

Solution

Along the plane: $mg\sin\theta = f_s$. Perpendicular: $N = mg\cos\theta$. Sliding begins when $f_s = \mu_s N$:

$$mg\sin\theta_c = \mu_s mg\cos\theta_c \implies \tan\theta_c = \mu_s.$$

2.5 Constraint Equations

When objects are connected by strings or rods, their accelerations are not independent. The constraint that string length is constant provides additional equations.

Example 2.3 — Double Atwood Machine

Mass $m_1$ hangs on one side of a pulley; on the other side hangs a second pulley with masses $m_2$ and $m_3$. Find the accelerations of all three masses.

Solution

Let $a_1$ be the acceleration of $m_1$ (downward positive) and $a'$ the acceleration of $m_2$ relative to the lower pulley (downward positive for $m_2$). Then $a_2 = -a_1 + a'$ and $a_3 = -a_1 - a'$.

Equations: $m_1g - T_1 = m_1 a_1$, $T_1 = 2T_2$ (massless lower pulley), $m_2 g - T_2 = m_2 a_2$, $m_3 g - T_2 = m_3 a_3$.

Solving this system yields:

$$a_1 = g\,\frac{4m_2 m_3 - m_1(m_2+m_3)}{4m_2 m_3 + m_1(m_2+m_3)}.$$

2.6 Non-Inertial Frames and Pseudo-Forces

Theorem 2.1 — Pseudo-Force in an Accelerating Frame

In a frame accelerating with $\mathbf{A}$ relative to an inertial frame, Newton's second law becomes:

$$m\mathbf{a}' = \mathbf{F}_{\text{real}} - m\mathbf{A}$$

where $\mathbf{a}'$ is the acceleration measured in the non-inertial frame. The term $-m\mathbf{A}$ is the pseudo-force (or inertial force).

Example 2.4 — Pendulum in an Accelerating Train

A pendulum hangs in a train accelerating at $a$ horizontally. Find the equilibrium angle.

Solution

In the train's frame, the pseudo-force is $ma$ backward. The effective gravity is $\mathbf{g}_{\text{eff}} = (-a, -g)$. The pendulum aligns with $\mathbf{g}_{\text{eff}}$, making angle $\theta = \arctan(a/g)$ with the vertical.

2.7 Circular Motion

Theorem 2.2 — Centripetal Acceleration

A particle moving at speed $v$ along a circular path of radius $R$ has centripetal acceleration

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{R} = \omega^2 R$$

directed toward the center. The net radial force must supply this: $F_{\text{net, radial}} = mv^2/R$.

Example 2.5 — Conical Pendulum

A mass $m$ moves in a horizontal circle at the end of a string of length $\ell$ making angle $\theta$ with the vertical. Find the period.

Solution

Vertical: $T\cos\theta = mg$. Radial: $T\sin\theta = m\omega^2 \ell\sin\theta$.

Dividing: $\omega^2 = g/(\ell\cos\theta)$, so $\displaystyle \tau = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\ell\cos\theta}{g}}$.

2.8 Exercises

Exercise 2.1

Three blocks of masses $m_1$, $m_2$, $m_3$ are connected by strings on a frictionless horizontal surface and pulled by a force $F$ applied to $m_1$. Find the tension in each string.

Exercise 2.2

A wedge of mass $M$ and angle $\alpha$ rests on a frictionless floor. A block of mass $m$ slides frictionlessly down the wedge. Find the acceleration of the wedge.

Exercise 2.3

A bead slides on a frictionless wire shaped as a parabola $y = x^2/(2R)$. Find the normal force at the vertex as a function of the bead's speed.

Exercise 2.4

A car rounds a banked curve of radius $R$ at angle $\theta$ with the horizontal. The coefficient of static friction is $\mu_s$. Find the maximum and minimum speeds for the car not to slip.
